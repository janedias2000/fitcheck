# Interview Synthesis — All 10 Interviews
**BUS 395: Venture Creation with AI | Jane Dias | June 2026**

---

## Overview

10 interviews across two rounds. Round 1 (interviews 1–5, June 7): problem discovery with women ages 20s–40s. Round 2 (interviews 6–10, June 2026): concept validation and Van Westendorp pricing with 5 new women ages 31–53.

---

## Round 1 Synthesis (Interviews 1–5)

### Core Problem Confirmed

Every interviewee struggled with the same fundamental question: *does this actually look good on me?* None had a reliable way to answer it alone.

### 1. External Validation Is the Universal Coping Mechanism

All 5 women cope with outfit uncertainty the same way: asking someone they trust. They send photos to husbands, sisters, moms, and friends. But the system is flawed:
- Sales associates are biased toward making a sale
- Even friends are sometimes "just trying to be nice" (Mya)
- The honest person (Mya's mom, Danai's husband) isn't available at the moment of decision

**The gap: needing honest feedback and reliably getting it are two different things.**

### 2. Online Shopping Amplifies the Problem

Every interviewee had unworn items from online purchases. The common thread wasn't wrong sizing — it was that things *fit* but didn't *look right* on their specific body. Returns are a workaround, not a solution (and not everyone uses them — Allison hates returning, Mya got stuck with non-returnable sale items).

### 3. Body Proportions Are the Real Pain Point

Multiple women described their body as hard to fit:
- Danai: smaller waist, bigger hips and thighs — pants fit "weird"
- Meg: narrow hips, thicker waist — opposite of "curvy" sizing
- Aly: pale complexion, many colors wash her out

Standard sizing doesn't account for proportions. Women largely figure this out through years of trial and error.

### 4. Style Knowledge Takes Years to Develop

Allison articulated what others implied: developing a reliable formula for dressing well took decades. Younger interviewees (Aly, Mya) are still in the trial-and-error phase. Women want a shortcut to knowing what works for their specific body.

### 5. Confidence Has Real Stakes

Low outfit confidence means:
- Not wanting to leave the house (Mya)
- Changing entirely before going out (Allison)
- Compensating with hair/makeup (Aly)
- Feeling sad after a shopping trip that yielded nothing (Danai)

### What Round 1 Disproved

| Assumption | Status |
|---|---|
| Women don't know their body type | Disproven — all 5 described their bodies precisely |
| Seeking second opinions signals self-doubt | Disproven — Allison (90% confident) still does it |
| Limited budget makes the problem worse | Partially — the mechanism is non-returnable items, which affects all income levels |

## Round 2 Synthesis (Interviews 6–10)

### Concept Validated

All 5 interviewees responded positively to the concept description. The strongest reactions came when they connected it to a specific moment they had experienced.

### Key New Findings

**1. The store-to-home shift is a distinct, high-signal pain point**
Zarina (interview 6) and Haley (interview 10) described loving something in store and then feeling differently at home. Danai in Round 1 also described this. This is a consistent third pain moment (alongside the fitting room and the pre-event getting-dressed moment) that the product addresses.

**2. The pre-event getting-dressed moment is high-stakes and underserved**
Daraja (interview 8) cycling through 4–5 outfits before something important is one of the highest-pain use cases. This moment is urgent, emotional, and happens when trusted people are least available.

**3. The problem doesn't disappear with experience**
Annie (interview 7), a former professional fashion stylist, still wants confirmation before going out. Even women who know all the rules want someone to say it looks good. This widens the potential market beyond women building their formula.

**4. Personalization to individual style matters**
Zoe (interview 9) raised the most important product design concern: she wants feedback that respects her aesthetic, not generic advice that pulls toward "safe." The profile-building feature is essential for this type of user.

**5. Framing value in terms of wasted spend is effective**
Haley (interview 10) immediately framed the $9/month price against what she already spends on items she never wears. "That's nothing compared to what I've wasted" is a naturally occurring comparison that could inform marketing.

### Pricing: Van Westendorp Results

| Interviewee | Too Cheap | Good Deal | Getting Expensive | Too Much | $9 Yes? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zarina (41) | <$5 | $12–15 | ~$20 | $30+ | Yes |
| Annie (53) | <$5 | $10–14 | ~$20 | $35+ | Yes |
| Daraja (31) | <$5 | $8–12 | ~$18 | $25+ | Yes |
| Zoe (31) | <$4 | $8–10 | ~$15 | $20+ | Yes |
| Haley (36) | <$8 | $15–20 | ~$25 | $40+ | Yes |

**All 5 said yes to $9.** Because all five said yes, $9 signals underpricing — if everyone says yes immediately, the price is below the market's actual ceiling.

**Indifference price (midpoint of good deal range) across all 5: approximately $12–14.**

**Decision: Raise price to $12/month.** This sits comfortably inside the "good deal" range for the majority, well below everyone's "too expensive" threshold, and avoids the quality-signal problem of pricing too low.

## Cross-Round Patterns (All 10 Interviews)

### What's Universal

1. **Photo-sending behavior** — every interviewee already takes photos of themselves in clothes and sends them to trusted people. The behavior exists; the product makes it more reliable and always available.

2. **The honest feedback problem** — every interviewee could name a person they trust for honest feedback. None of them could reliably access that person at the moment of decision.

3. **Returns as a downstream workaround, not a solution** — returns handle the aftermath of a bad purchase decision but don't prevent the decision. The product addresses the decision moment.

### What Varies by Segment

| Factor | 22–35 building formula (Aly, Mya, Zoe, Daraja) | Life transition (Meg, Daraja) | Self-solved (Allison, Annie) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily confidence | 40–50% | Low to moderate | 80–90% |
| Willingness to pay | Budget-sensitive | Moderate | Higher ceiling |
| Urgency | High | High | Moderate |
| Best use case | Fitting room, casual shopping | Getting dressed for events | Confirmation, not discovery |

### The Primary Target

Women 22–35 who are either:
- Building their style formula for the first time (Aly, Mya, Zoe)
- Back at square one after a life transition broke their formula (Daraja, Meg)

These women have the highest pain, the most active photo-sending behavior, and the least reliable access to honest feedback.

---

## Implications for Fit Check

1. **Price at $12/month** — supported by Van Westendorp data; below everyone's "too expensive" threshold and inside most interviewees' "good deal" range.

2. **Lead with the moment, not the product** — "you're in a fitting room and you can't tell" resonates more than "AI styling feedback."

3. **Don't promise body image improvement** — Meg named this explicitly as a different layer. Overselling causes churn.

4. **The profile must learn individual style** — Zoe's concern is valid. If the AI defaults to generic, it fails the customer with a strong aesthetic.

5. **The pre-event moment is a secondary product use case** — Daraja's 4-outfit cycling is a different trigger than the fitting room; both should be in the product frame.
